24 Years of Municipal Finance

Python · pandas · Jupyter

Wisconsin’s towns, cities, and villages each file an annual Municipal Financial Report (MFR). Two decades of those filings are a goldmine for anyone studying local government—but they arrive as a sprawl of inconsistent Excel and legacy spreadsheet files, with schemas that drift from year to year, data for a single year split across multiple sheets, and no common key to tie them together. Before any analysis is possible, someone has to make them speak the same language.

This project is that essential work: a Python pipeline that turns 24 years of messy MFR files into one clean, analysis-ready dataset.

What the pipeline does

  • Ingests a directory of mixed .xls / .xlsx files spanning 1998–2021, filtering out the noise (stray docx, zip, and misc files).
  • Normalizes each year’s idiosyncratic layout into a consistent structure, including cleaning up messy file and field names.
  • Stitches together years whose data is scattered across several sheets into a single record per municipality-year.
  • Combines every year into one long, tidy data structure keyed to a county-and-type mapping, so municipalities can be compared across time and geography.

Why it matters

Reliable public-policy analysis lives or dies on the quality of the underlying data. By making decades of fragmented municipal filings queryable in one place, this pipeline turns a pile of spreadsheets into something a researcher can actually ask questions of—how local revenues, expenditures, and tax rolls shift over time and differ between towns, villages, and cities. It’s the kind of reproducible data-engineering groundwork that makes everything downstream possible.
