About

I’m an environmental scientist and spatial-data analyst who works at the seam between fieldwork, satellite imagery, and the stories landscapes carry.

My work moves across a few registers that all feed one another. On the technical side, I build reproducible geospatial tools—most recently an open Google Earth Engine workflow for detecting winter livestock-waste applications on snow, published in Remote Sensing Applications: Society and Environment. On the research side, I study how environmental professionals see and value the landscapes they manage, which was the subject of my master’s thesis on urban forestry in Austin and Denver.

I hold a Master of Science in Environment and Resources from the Nelson Institute for Environmental Studies at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, and I’ve collaborated with the Environmental Law & Policy Center on satellite-based environmental monitoring. Alongside the academic and technical work, I map and write about the prairies of southern Wisconsin and grow rare native plants from seed—documenting the process, successes and failures alike, on my blog the Plant Propagation Project.

What ties it together is a conviction that seeing a landscape clearly—whether through a classifier, a historic plat map, or a season spent coaxing gentian seeds to germinate—is the first step toward caring for it well.

What I work with

Remote sensing & geospatial: Google Earth Engine, Sentinel-2 & Landsat imagery, cloud/snow/cropland masking, design-based accuracy assessment, GeoPandas, QGIS-style cartography.
Data science: Python (pandas), data cleaning and pipeline design, Jupyter, reproducible workflows.
Research & writing: qualitative fieldwork and interviews, environmental history and archival mapping, science communication for general audiences.
