About

I’m an environmental scientist and geospatial data engineer who works at the intersection of applied conservation, remote sensing, and environmental history.

On the technical side, I build reproducible geospatial tools; most recently, I built an open Google Earth Engine workflow for detecting winter livestock-waste applications on snow, published in Remote Sensing Applications: Society and Environment. On the theoretical side, I study how environmental professionals see and value the landscapes they manage, which was the subject of my master’s thesis on urban forestry in Austin and Denver. I’m also a co-author of “Great Green Walls: Hype, Myth, and Science” (Annual Review of Environment and Resources, 2023), a critical peer-reviewed review of large-scale afforestation programs across Africa and China.

I hold an M.S. in Environment and Resources from the University of Wisconsin–Madison’s Nelson Institute for Environmental Studies and a B.A. in Mathematics from Carleton College. I’ve collaborated with the Environmental Law & Policy Center on satellite-based environmental monitoring projects, and I’ve worked for Forrester Research synthesizing data and AI research for a corporate audience. Alongside the academic and technical work, I map and write about the prairies of southern Wisconsin and grow rare native plants from seed, documenting the process, successes and failures alike, on my blog the Plant Propagation Project.

What I work with

Remote sensing & geospatial: Google Earth Engine (Python & JavaScript), QGIS, ArcGIS, Envi, Sentinel-2 & Landsat imagery.
Data science: Python (pandas, geopandas), data cleaning and pipeline design, Jupyter, reproducible workflows.
Research & writing: qualitative fieldwork and interviews, environmental history and archival research, science communication for general audiences.
